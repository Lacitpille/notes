Writing rigorous mathematics is slow. The gap between *noticing something interesting* and *publishing a post that states it correctly, motivates it well, and connects it to the broader literature* involves a kind of labor that is easy to postpone indefinitely.

The pipeline below is my attempt to automate the scaffolding around that labor — so the only irreplaceable step is the one that requires genuine mathematical judgment.

## The core problem

A typical math post fails at one of three points:

1. **It never starts.** The seed observation sits in a scratch file, unstructured, and the activation energy to turn it into a real draft feels too high.
2. **It stays shallow.** The first draft captures the idea but doesn't stress-test it, doesn't find the connections, doesn't ask whether this is actually true in the edge cases.
3. **It loses your voice.** AI-assisted writing converges toward a certain fluent blandness; the post is technically correct but says nothing that couldn't have been said by anyone.

A multi-agent pipeline doesn't solve these problems — but it can change where your attention has to go. The SEED stage lowers the activation energy. The DEEPEN and CRITIQUE stages do the stress-testing. And because *you* sit at both ends (seed and final gate), the voice problem stays in your control.

## Why context accumulation matters

The single most important file in the repo structure is `context/context.md`. Each run of the pipeline reads it before doing anything; each publish step updates it. Over time it becomes a live index of:

- results you've established (with post links)
- open questions you're tracking
- notation decisions you've standardized
- themes and how they connect

This is what makes the DIVERGE stage useful rather than generic. When the agent fans out from a new idea, it does so against *your* accumulated work — not against the general mathematical literature in the abstract. It can surface connections like "this is the same universal property argument you used in your 2-pullback post, modulo strictification."

Without that context file, you get a capable assistant with no memory. With it, you get something closer to a collaborator that has read everything you've written.

## The critique phase

The CRITIQUE stage runs in a **separate Claude session with fresh context**. This is deliberate. An agent that helped write the draft will defend it; an agent that reads it cold will find the gaps.

The critique agent reads `context/quality-rubric.md` — a file you write and maintain — which encodes what you actually care about:

- Is the central claim stated precisely, or does it gesture at precision?
- Are the examples the right examples, or just the obvious ones?
- Does the post say something, or does it demonstrate that something can be said?

The rubric is the part that cannot be automated. It is the distillation of your mathematical taste.

## On the formalization step

The DEEPEN stage includes an optional Lean 4 formalization pass. Not every post warrants it — informal arguments about $(\infty,1)$-categories are not always productively formalizable — but the *attempt* is often the most useful part of the stage.

When formalization fails, it usually fails because something is vaguer than it appeared. The type-checker is a very patient adversarial reader. Even a partial formalization — getting the key definitions into Lean, leaving sorry placeholders for the hard parts — forces a precision that prose can evade.

## Practical notes

The pipeline is not meant to be run end-to-end in one sitting. The natural rhythm is:

1. Drop seeds continuously, as you notice things
2. Run REFINE + DIVERGE in a single session when you have an hour
3. Come back to DEEPEN + CRITIQUE with fresh eyes
4. Final gate is a real editorial pass — read it, change what your voice would change

The agents do the work that does not require being you. The work that requires being you remains yours.
